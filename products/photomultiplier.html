    Technical Description

    The PMT consists of a photocathode that emits photoelectrons when struck by photons. These electrons are accelerated toward a series of dynodes, each maintained at progressively higher potentials. At each dynode, secondary electrons are emitted, amplifying the signal in a chain reaction until a final measurable current is collected at the anode.

    Core Components

    • Photocathode: Converts incident photons into photoelectrons via the photoelectric effect.
    • Dynodes: Electrode stages that multiply electrons through secondary emission.
    • Anode: Collects the multiplied electrons to produce output current.
    • Vacuum Envelope: Maintains a vacuum environment for free electron movement.
    • Voltage Divider: Provides the required bias voltages to dynodes.

    Advantages

    • Extremely high sensitivity to light
    • Fast response time
    • Low noise and excellent signal-to-noise ratio

    Applications

    • Scintillation detectors in nuclear and particle physics
    • Medical imaging (e.g., PET scanners)
    • Fluorescence spectroscopy
    • Low-light level detection in astrophysics

    Safety Notes

    • Handle high-voltage circuits with caution.
    • Protect PMTs from exposure to strong light, which may damage the photocathode.
    • Ensure vacuum integrity to avoid implosion risk.
